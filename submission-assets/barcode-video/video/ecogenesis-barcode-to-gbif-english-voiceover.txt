Imagine that a researcher has a water sample, a soil sample, or a lab barcode result.
The computer returns a species name, and the temptation is simple: copy the top hit and publish it as a biodiversity record.

But for GBIF, and for science, that is not enough.
A DNA match can be short, ambiguous, shared by several species, unsupported by a reference library, or blocked by missing occurrence metadata.
The result may be useful, but it may not be safe to call it a species-level occurrence.

EcoGenesis turns that risky moment into a clear workflow.
The project is a Molecular Evidence Conversion and Repair Engine for GBIF.
Its first working layer is the Barcode-to-GBIF Evidence Compiler.
It does not replace GBIF Sequence ID, BLAST, BOLD, UNITE, or a laboratory pipeline.
Instead, it receives their CSV-style results and asks a more practical question:
what can we safely claim, what must be downgraded, and what must be fixed before publication?

For a normal user, the workflow is simple.
Open the compiler, upload a CSV file, validate the rows, generate the evidence package, inspect the decisions, and download the exports.
The required columns are sequence ID and sequence.
Match columns such as top taxon, identity, coverage, a second competing hit, barcode gap, and diagnostic k-mers unlock stronger decisions.
If those match results are missing, the tool does not invent confidence.
It keeps the record in review.

The output is intentionally human-readable.
Some records are safe to publish as species.
Some are safe only at genus or higher rank.
Some are weak because coverage or identity is not enough.
Some are taxonomically strong, but not publishable yet because fields like occurrence ID, event date, marker, reference database, or method are missing.

This is the key difference.
The tool separates taxonomic evidence from publication readiness.
A good DNA match is not automatically a GBIF-ready record.
And a blocked publication record is not thrown away; it becomes a repair task.

The method is deterministic and visible.
Species-level output is allowed only when fixed gates pass:
identity, coverage, ambiguity, lowest common ancestor, barcode gap, diagnostic k-mer support, GBIF metadata, and DNA-derived metadata.
If a sequence can belong to multiple species, the compiler downgrades the claim to the safest shared taxonomic level.
If the data cannot support absence, trend, true distribution, or phenotype claims, those claims are blocked.

The platform also includes a GBIF occurrence-audit layer.
That layer checks GBIF API access in live network mode, uses GBIF-mediated occurrence records, and demonstrates safe language for evidence context, sampling gaps, empty cells, and citation readiness.
It is not used as hidden molecular evidence.
It is a separate audit showing how the same claim-safety philosophy works on GBIF occurrence data.

For users, EcoGenesis reduces mistakes and gives a clear next action.
For data publishers, it turns messy molecular outputs into Darwin Core templates, DNA-derived extension templates, publication blockers, methods text, citations, and an evidence graph.
For GBIF, it helps convert more DNA-derived observations into reusable, better documented, and more honestly described biodiversity evidence.

The goal is not to say that the tool knows nature perfectly.
The goal is to stop overclaiming and increase useful publication.
Instead of asking, "What is the top hit?", EcoGenesis asks:
"What is the safest claim supported by this evidence, and what repair will make more records GBIF-ready?"

That is the platform shift:
from isolated DNA matches to reproducible evidence conversion,
from uncertainty hidden in a score to explicit blockers,
and from a raw spreadsheet to a publishable evidence package.
